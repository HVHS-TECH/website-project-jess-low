Plague

Overview:

The plague is an infectious disease, commonly spread by flea bites or handling an infected animal, though the pneumonic strain can be spread by particles in the air. Many people don’t realise it, but plague is still around to this day.


Types:

There are three types of plague: bubonic, pneumonic, and septicemic. Bubonic plague is the most common and affects the lymph nodes, which are structures that fight infection and disease. Deaths typically occur in about ten days if left untreated. Pneumonic plague infects the lungs and is considered the deadliest due to its ability to infect via airborne particles. Without treatment, the victim would die within 24 hours of symptoms showing. Septicemic plague targets the blood, and can also kill within 24 hours of symptoms. All last for a few weeks if treated.


Symptoms:

Bubonic plague targets the buboes, also known as lymph nodes. Its symptoms include fever, headaches, and swollen buboes found near the groin, armpits, or neck. If untreated, bubonic plague can progress to pneumonic, septicemic, or even both plagues. It typically has a fatality rate of 5-15% if treated, and 30-60% if untreated.

Pneumonic plague targets the lungs. Its symptoms include fever, headaches, chest pain, nausea, and coughing, usually producing bloody or watery mucus. If untreated, it can cause shock and respiratory failure. It typically has a fatality rate of 30-50% if treated, and almost 100% if untreated.

Septicemic plague targets the bloodstream. Its symptoms include fever, nausea, shock, bleeding under the skin and from the mouth, nose, or rectum, organ failure, and skin necrosis in areas such as fingers, toes, and nose. On occasion, it can be asymptomatic. It typically has a fatality rate of 15-30% if treated, and almost 100% if untreated.


Pandemics:

There have been three major plague pandemics. The Plague of Justinian, the Black Death, and the unoriginally named Third Plague pandemic. The most deadly and well-known one was the Black Death. The total amount of deaths is debated, but it was estimated to be between 25 million and 200 million. It was named Black Death due to how patients’ skin would turn black due to skin necrosis. Both the Black Death and the Plague of Justinian are considered to be in the top 3 of most dangerous pandemics, with the Third Plague pandemic being top 10.


Nursery Rhyme:

There is some debate as to whether the 'Ring a Ring O' Rosie' nursery rhyme is about the plague or not, but there are some good analyses as to why it could be. One of the older versions of the rhyme goes like this:

"Ring-a-round the rosie,

A pocket full of posies,

Ashes! Ashes!

We all fall down."

It is believed the first line references the red rashes commonly found on plague victims, and the second is about the flowers people believed would protect them. The third line varies between 'A-tishoo! A-tishoo!' and 'Ashes! Ashes!', with 'Ashes!' referring to the bodies' cremation or the burning of victims' houses.
